Communicating for Learning: Explaining Neural Networks Simply

Einstein's bar is a good one to fail against first. My first attempt asked Claude to "explain neural networks" with no constraints, and the result was accurate but useless for learning: "A neural network is a computational model composed of layers of interconnected nodes, or neurons, that apply weighted transformations and non-linear activation functions to input data, adjusting weights via backpropagation to minimize a loss function." Technically correct, but it violates almost every principle from this week's lesson. There's no chunking, no progressive disclosure, no story, and no relevance to anyone who isn't already close to understanding the concept. It's the equivalent of explaining a car engine by handing someone the parts list.

So I rewrote the prompt to explicitly demand the communication strategies from the lesson: "Explain what a neural network is using a simple real-world analogy, in three short stages that build from the simplest idea to slightly more detail, with no jargon in the first stage." Here's what that produced:

Stage 1: The simple idea: Imagine you're learning to recognize your friend's knock on the door. At first, you don't know the pattern. But every time they knock and you open the door and it's actually them, you get a little better at recognizing "that's their knock." A neural network learns the same way, by guessing, checking if it was right, and adjusting.

Stage 2: Adding a bit more: A neural network isn't just one guess; it's a chain of small guesses stacked in layers. The first layer notices basic clues (like "loud" or "fast"), the next layer combines those clues into bigger patterns ("that rhythm sounds familiar"), and the last layer makes the final call ("yes, that's them"). Each layer refines what the layer before it noticed.

Stage 3:  The mechanism underneath: Each connection between "clue-noticers" has a dial on it, called a weight, that controls how much attention that clue gets. Every time the network gets it wrong, it nudges those dials slightly, the way you'd adjust an old radio antenna to get a clearer signal. Do that millions of times and the dials settle into a pattern that gets it right almost every time.

I applied three lesson concepts deliberately. Chunking split the concept into three digestible stages instead of one dense paragraph. Progressive disclosure meant stage one has zero jargon and each stage after it earns the right to add one new term (layers, then weights) only after the prior idea landed. Storytelling used the door-knock scenario as a throughline the whole explanation returns to, rather than switching analogies mid-explanation, which the lesson notes helps retention. A visual aid would strengthen this further, a simple three-panel diagram showing the knock sound going through three layers of "clue boxes" before reaching a yes/no answer, which I did not generate here but would pair well with this explanation for a visual learner.
